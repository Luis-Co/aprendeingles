Lesson 3: The Imperative

Level: A1
Length: 60 min
Topic: Giving orders, advice & directions
Score: 0%

Lesson Goals

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Form affirmative and negative imperatives
  • Use imperatives for instructions, warnings, recipes and directions
  • Soften commands with please and let's
  • Understand public-sign imperatives (EXIT, DON'T WALK, etc.)

Part A: Explanation & Examples

1. What is the imperative?

The imperative is the base form of the verb without to.
We use it to tell people what to do.

Affirmative Negative Example
Sit down. Don't sit down. Please sit down.
Be quiet. Don't be quiet. Don't be late.

Key Points:

  • No subject is written, but we mean you.
  • Please at the beginning or end makes it politer.
  • Let's + base verb includes the speaker (suggestion).
    Let's go! Let's not wait.

2. Spelling & verb quirks

  • Base form = infinitive without to: open, close, listen
  • Verb be → Be careful! Don't be silly!
  • Add tag for intonation check: Open the window, will you?

3. Typical contexts

Signs / notices

EXIT, PUSH, PULL, DON'T ENTER, NO SMOKING

Directions

Go straight on, turn left, cross the road

Recipes

Mix the eggs, add salt, bake for 20 minutes

Safety & warnings

Watch out! Don't touch the wire!

Classroom language

Open your books, stand up, sit down, please

4. Softening strategies

  • Please + imperative → Please wait here.
  • Imperative + please → Wait here, please.
  • Let's = suggestion → Let's take a break.
  • Why don't we…? (A2+) → Why don't we start?

5. Common errors

❌ Wrong ✅ Correct
To sit here. Sit here.
Don't to shout. Don't shout.
Let's to go. Let's go.

Part E: Self-Study Roadmap

Grammar Focus

  • Practice forming affirmative and negative imperatives

  • Study how to soften commands with polite language
  • Master the use of "let's" for suggestions

Vocabulary

  • Learn common imperative verbs for directions
  • Study imperative phrases used in recipes
  • Practice safety warnings and classroom language

Skills Practice

  • Reading: identify imperatives in instructions and signs
  • Listening: notice imperative usage in directions and warnings
  • Speaking: give clear instructions using imperatives
  • Writing: write simple recipes or directions using imperatives
